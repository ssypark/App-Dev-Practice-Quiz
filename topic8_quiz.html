Topic 8: Nervous Tissue Quiz

1. What are the two major divisions of the nervous system?

  • A) Central and peripheral nervous systems
  • B) Somatic and autonomic nervous systems
  • C) Sensory and motor nervous systems
  • D) Sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems
Answer: A) Central and peripheral nervous systems
Explanation: The central nervous system (CNS) consists of the brain and spinal cord, while the peripheral nervous system (PNS) includes all other neural structures.

2. True or False: Neurons are the only type of cell found in nervous tissue.

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: B) False
Explanation: Nervous tissue contains neurons and neuroglia (supporting cells that provide structural and functional support to neurons).

3. What are the structural components of a neuron? (Select all that apply)

  • A) Dendrites
  • B) Axon
  • C) Soma (cell body)
  • D) Myofibrils
Answer: A, B, C
Explanation: A neuron consists of dendrites (receiving signals), an axon (transmitting signals), and the soma (cell body, containing the nucleus).

4. True or False: Myelin sheaths increase the speed of nerve impulse conduction.

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: A) True
Explanation: Myelin sheaths insulate axons and enable faster conduction of electrical impulses through saltatory conduction.

5. Which type of neuroglia is responsible for producing myelin in the CNS?

  • A) Schwann cells
  • B) Oligodendrocytes
  • C) Astrocytes
  • D) Microglia
Answer: B) Oligodendrocytes
Explanation: Oligodendrocytes produce myelin sheaths in the central nervous system, insulating multiple axons.

6. What is the primary function of the axon in a neuron?

  • A) Receiving signals from other neurons
  • B) Transmitting signals to other cells
  • C) Synthesizing neurotransmitters
  • D) Protecting the neuron
Answer: B) Transmitting signals to other cells
Explanation: The axon transmits electrical impulses from the neuron to other cells, including other neurons, muscles, or glands.

7. True or False: Schwann cells produce myelin in the peripheral nervous system (PNS).

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: A) True
Explanation: Schwann cells produce myelin sheaths in the PNS, which insulate individual axons and speed up nerve conduction.

8. What is the name of the gaps in the myelin sheath along an axon?

  • A) Synaptic clefts
  • B) Nodes of Ranvier
  • C) Dendritic spines
  • D) Axon terminals
Answer: B) Nodes of Ranvier
Explanation: Nodes of Ranvier are small gaps between myelin segments that facilitate rapid nerve impulse conduction through saltatory conduction.

9. Which type of neuroglia is involved in phagocytosis in the CNS?

  • A) Astrocytes
  • B) Oligodendrocytes
  • C) Microglia
  • D) Ependymal cells
Answer: C) Microglia
Explanation: Microglia act as the immune cells of the CNS, removing debris, pathogens, and damaged cells through phagocytosis.

10. True or False: The resting membrane potential of a neuron is typically around -70 mV.

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: A) True
Explanation: The resting membrane potential of a neuron is approximately -70 mV, due to the difference in ion concentrations across the cell membrane.

11. Which ion is primarily responsible for depolarization during an action potential?

  • A) Potassium (K+)
  • B) Sodium (Na+)
  • C) Calcium (Ca2+)
  • D) Chloride (Cl-)
Answer: B) Sodium (Na+)
Explanation: During depolarization, sodium channels open, allowing sodium ions to flow into the neuron, increasing the membrane potential.

12. True or False: Action potentials decrease in strength as they travel along an axon.

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: B) False
Explanation: Action potentials are all-or-none signals that maintain their strength as they travel along the axon.

13. What is the primary role of astrocytes in the CNS?

  • A) Producing myelin
  • B) Forming the blood-brain barrier
  • C) Conducting nerve impulses
  • D) Phagocytosis
Answer: B) Forming the blood-brain barrier
Explanation: Astrocytes contribute to the blood-brain barrier by regulating the passage of substances between blood vessels and neural tissue.

14. What occurs at the synaptic cleft during synaptic transmission?

  • A) Electrical signals are directly transmitted to the next neuron.
  • B) Neurotransmitters are released into the cleft.
  • C) Myelin sheaths are reabsorbed.
  • D) Sodium ions are released into the cleft.
Answer: B) Neurotransmitters are released into the cleft.
Explanation: During synaptic transmission, neurotransmitters are released from the presynaptic neuron into the synaptic cleft and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic neuron.

15. True or False: Ependymal cells help circulate cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: A) True
Explanation: Ependymal cells line the ventricles of the brain and the central canal of the spinal cord, helping to circulate cerebrospinal fluid.

16. Which neurotransmitter is most commonly associated with the neuromuscular junction?

  • A) Dopamine
  • B) Acetylcholine
  • C) Serotonin
  • D) GABA
Answer: B) Acetylcholine
Explanation: Acetylcholine is the primary neurotransmitter at the neuromuscular junction, facilitating communication between neurons and muscle cells.

17. True or False: Hyperpolarization occurs when the membrane potential becomes more negative than the resting potential.

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: A) True
Explanation: Hyperpolarization makes the membrane potential more negative, often following repolarization, during an action potential.

18. Which part of the neuron integrates incoming signals and initiates an action potential?

  • A) Dendrites
  • B) Axon hillock
  • C) Axon terminal
  • D) Soma
Answer: B) Axon hillock
Explanation: The axon hillock is where incoming signals are summed, and if the threshold is reached, an action potential is initiated.

19. True or False: The sympathetic nervous system is responsible for the "rest and digest" response.

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: B) False
Explanation: The sympathetic nervous system is responsible for the "fight or flight" response, while the parasympathetic system manages "rest and digest" activities.

20. What is the role of voltage-gated sodium channels during an action potential?

  • A) They close during depolarization.
  • B) They open during depolarization, allowing sodium ions to enter the cell.
  • C) They open during repolarization, allowing potassium ions to exit the cell.
  • D) They maintain the resting potential.
Answer: B) They open during depolarization, allowing sodium ions to enter the cell.
Explanation: Voltage-gated sodium channels open in response to a change in membrane potential, initiating depolarization by allowing sodium ions to flow into the neuron.

21. True or False: The parasympathetic nervous system decreases heart rate.

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: A) True
Explanation: The parasympathetic nervous system slows down heart rate as part of its "rest and digest" functions.

22. Which ion is primarily responsible for repolarization during an action potential?

  • A) Sodium (Na+)
  • B) Potassium (K+)
  • C) Calcium (Ca2+)
  • D) Chloride (Cl-)
Answer: B) Potassium (K+)
Explanation: During repolarization, potassium channels open, allowing potassium ions to leave the neuron, restoring the negative membrane potential.

23. Which structure in the nervous system acts as a relay center for sensory information?

  • A) Hypothalamus
  • B) Thalamus
  • C) Cerebellum
  • D) Brainstem
Answer: B) Thalamus
Explanation: The thalamus processes and relays sensory information to the appropriate areas of the cerebral cortex.

24. True or False: Saltatory conduction occurs in unmyelinated axons.

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: B) False
Explanation: Saltatory conduction occurs in myelinated axons, where action potentials jump between Nodes of Ranvier, increasing conduction speed.

25. What type of neuron transmits impulses from sensory receptors to the central nervous system?

  • A) Motor neurons
  • B) Interneurons
  • C) Sensory neurons
  • D) Glial cells
Answer: C) Sensory neurons
Explanation: Sensory neurons carry information from sensory receptors to the CNS for processing.

26. Which part of the brain is responsible for coordinating voluntary movements and balance?

  • A) Cerebrum
  • B) Brainstem
  • C) Cerebellum
  • D) Thalamus
Answer: C) Cerebellum
Explanation: The cerebellum coordinates voluntary movements and maintains balance and posture.

27. True or False: The dorsal root of a spinal nerve contains motor neurons.

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: B) False
Explanation: The dorsal root contains sensory neurons, while the ventral root contains motor neurons.

28. What is the primary role of the hypothalamus in the nervous system?

  • A) Processing sensory information
  • B) Regulating homeostasis
  • C) Controlling voluntary movements
  • D) Transmitting motor signals
Answer: B) Regulating homeostasis
Explanation: The hypothalamus regulates homeostasis, including temperature control, hunger, thirst, and hormone release.

29. Which glial cells form the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)?

  • A) Astrocytes
  • B) Microglia
  • C) Ependymal cells
  • D) Schwann cells
Answer: C) Ependymal cells
Explanation: Ependymal cells line the ventricles and central canal, producing and circulating cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).

30. True or False: Interneurons are found only in the central nervous system.

  • A) True
  • B) False
Answer: A) True
Explanation: Interneurons are located entirely within the CNS, where they integrate and process information.
